PROMPT Method of Responding to Problem Behavior

Know & See:

Read More about how to Know & See Establishing with students

The PROMPT method gives you a blueprint from how to respond to problem behaviors as they happen during a lesson.

The first step is to remember that irritating, disruptive, and aggravating problem behaviors are a “cause to pause.” This means to think before acting.

  1. Proximity control: Simply moving actively through the classroom can help minimize problem behaviors because the students know that you can see them. If you see a student start to behave inappropriately, stand next to him or her. Research shows that proximity control reduces problem behavior by up to 40-50%.
  2. Redirection: If standing next to the student does not stop the behavior, offer a redirection. Ask him or her to perform a simple task that you know he or she is not likely to refuse. For example, you can ask the student to hand out papers, take a note to a fellow teacher, or any other routine task. Research shows that proximity control with redirection reduces problem behavior by up to 80%.
  3. Ongoing Monitoring: After redirecting a student, continue to monitor his or her behavior. If you notice the student staying on task, give positive reinforcement, such as a simple smile, or a recognition that he or she is working hard. This positive reinforcement makes the student more likely to continue to stay on task and not repeat the problem behavior.
  4. Prompt Expected Behavior: If ongoing monitoring shows that the student continues to display the problem behavior, prompt the behavior that you expect. This prompt is a verbal command saying exactly what you want the student to do. The prompt is positively stated, asks for only one behavior, and is a statement rather than a question. For example, “I need you to start working on your essay by writing the first sentence.”
  5. Teaching Interaction: If prompting the expected behavior does not work to correct the problem, use a teaching interaction. A teaching interaction is a private, one-on-one conversation with the student in which you express empathy, label the inappropriate behavior, describe the appropriate alternative behavior, provide a rationale, deliver a warning of a consequence, and deliver private feedback or praise. Research shows that all the steps of the PROMPT sequence together will stop 95% of problem behaviors.
