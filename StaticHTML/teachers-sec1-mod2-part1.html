The Big Idea Explained

Most of us would engage in the same problem behaviors as our students if we sat in on our own classrooms. Have you ever attended an all day training and texted or talked to your neighbor about something unrelated to the topic at hand? How would you feel if the speaker yelled at you to shut up or told you to leave the room? Probably not too good.

All day trainings can be painful, and just think, this is what we ask students to do 5 days per week.

Research shows that punitive discipline often produces negative side effects, such as the student either fighting, becoming defiant, or withdrawing by avoiding work, you, or the class altogether. In essence, punitive practices can harm your relationship with the student, which makes it more likely the student will misbehave in the future.

The reality is that the vast majority of problem behaviors in the classroom are irritating or disruptive, but they are normal behaviors that even well-functioning adults, like you, would exhibit under similar situations.

The question is how you can stop those problem behaviors before they interfere with learning and lead to the student being removed from the classroom?

This module on PROMPT shows step-by-step practices that you can use to stop a problem behavior or resolve it without waiting for the behavior to become disruptive or damaging your long-term relationship with the student.
